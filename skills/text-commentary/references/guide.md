# Text Analysis Assignment Guidelines (Core Standards)

This document serves as the primary guide for philosophical text commentary, derived from course assignment guidelines. All skill outputs must adhere to these standards as the highest criterion.

## 1. Purpose of Commentary

Writing commentary is preparation for writing papers and reports. Upon completing the commentary, one can then prepare to write a thesis or report.

## 2. What is Commentary

Analysis of an important passage extracted from a text. This analysis is essential for understanding the text, grasping its structure, and recognizing its significance.

## 3. Preparatory Work Before Writing Commentary

### 3.1 Close Reading of the Text
Precisely and clearly understand the meaning of the text.

### 3.2 Mark Logical Connectors
Mark "logical connectors" that appear in the text, such as "because," "thus," "therefore," "but," etc. These help isolate the original text's structure and construct the concepts for the commentary.

### 3.3 Critical Prohibition
**Do not paraphrase the text on your own, and do not deviate from the subject matter.**

## 4. How to Understand a Text

- **4.1 Theme**: What is the theme of the text? What does it discuss?
- **4.2 Purpose**: What is the purpose of the text? What is the author trying to do?
- **4.3 Logical Structure**: What is the logical structure of the text?

## 5. Four Common Text Types

- **(a)** Guides the reader to correct a certain view, or even to abandon it completely
- **(b)** Seeks to reject a certain claim, possibly simultaneously revealing certain erroneous principles underlying that claim, or revealing contradictions within that claim, or revealing certain facts not analyzed by that claim
- **(c)** Attempts to establish a definition
- **(d)** Attempts to solve a certain problem

## 6. Three Parts of Commentary

### 6.1 Introduction

- **6.1.1** Present the problem, that is, the problem the text seeks to solve
- **6.1.2** It is absolutely unnecessary to analyze the author's biography; nor is it necessary to provide an overview of the author's thinking
- **6.1.3** It is also absolutely inappropriate to begin analysis from a concept; instead, begin from the text itself

### 6.2 Development

- **6.2.1** Begin with universal concepts mentioned within the text's concerns
- **6.2.2** Introduce the problem
- **6.2.3** Under the form of the problem presented by the text (question), present the commentator's own problem (problem)
- **6.2.4** Emphasize the core concern of the problem

### 6.3 Conclusion

- **6.3.1** Draw a conclusion about the results already obtained
- **6.3.2** Expand the results or open toward new problems
