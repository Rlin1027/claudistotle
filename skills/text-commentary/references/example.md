## Logical Connectors Marked

"That kings should philosophize, or philosophers become kings, is not to be expected. Nor indeed is it to be desired, ==because== the possession of power inevitably corrupts the free judgment of reason. ==However==, that kings ==or== the people governing themselves according to laws of equality should not let the class of philosophers disappear or remain silent, ==but== instead permit them to speak publicly, is quite indispensable to both for the enlightenment of their affairs; ==moreover==, since this class, by its nature, is incapable of forming any league or faction, there ==is== no danger of its propagating pernicious delusions." --- Kant, "Perpetual Peace"

## Connector Analysis

| Connector | Category | Argumentative Function |
|-----------|----------|------------------------|
| or (1st instance) | Contrast | Presents two unrealistic scenarios (kings philosophizing vs. philosophers becoming kings), showing both sides of the problem |
| nor is it to be desired | Progression | Reinforces the same proposition, emphasizing both situations are equally impossible |
| because | Causal | Explains the fundamental reason why the above is not to be expected: power inevitably corrupts reason |
| However | Turning Point | Key argumentative pivot, shifting from "what is impossible" to "what is necessary" |
| or (2nd instance) | Contrast | References both "kings" and "people governing themselves," showing universal applicability of the principle |
| but instead permit | Contrast/Turning Point | Corresponds to "not let disappear or remain silent," presenting the positive position (they should be permitted to speak) |
| moreover | Progression | Adds a second reason, further supporting the necessity |
| is (resulting in) | Causal | Derives the conclusion of "no danger of propagating pernicious delusions" from "incapable of forming any league or faction" |

## Preliminary Text Analysis

**Theme:** The relationship between kings and philosophers; the tension between power and philosophical freedom; how rulers should treat philosophy.

**Purpose:** Kant argues that although it is unrealistic for kings to practice philosophy themselves or for philosophers to become kings, rulers should protect and permit philosophers to speak freely. The author defends the independence and freedom of philosophy while showing this also benefits the rulers themselves.

**Logical Structure:**
1. Premise: Neither kings thinking philosophically nor philosophers becoming kings is to be expected (because power inevitably corrupts reason)
2. Turning Point: Yet rulers should permit philosophers to speak publicly
3. Supporting Reasons: (a) Both parties need philosophers to understand their affairs; (b) Philosophers are by nature incapable of forming factions, posing no political threat
4. Conclusion: Philosophical freedom is both necessary and harmless to the political order

**Text Type:** (d) — solving a problem. Kant addresses the practical question "How should rulers treat philosophy and philosophers?" by offering a solution.

## Example Two: Augustine's Confessions, Book II, Chapter 4

## Logical Connectors Marked

"Lord, your law punishes theft, a law written in human hearts that sin itself cannot erase. Which thief willingly permits another thief to steal from him? Which rich man patiently allows a poor man, driven by need, to steal from him?
==Yet== I desired to steal, and I did it, ==not== driven by necessity or hunger, ==but== from a failure of virtue, weary of justice, and full of wickedness. ==Because== what I stole, I already possessed in greater abundance and of better quality. I did not desire to enjoy what I stole, ==but== only to enjoy the theft and the sin itself.
Near my father's vineyard stood a pear tree, laden with fruit that was neither beautiful in appearance nor pleasant to taste. Late one night, a group of us young ruffians, after playing in the streets until late, went and shook down and carried off all the pears we could. We brought away large baskets full of them, ==not== to eat ourselves, ==but== to throw to the pigs. We merely tasted a few ourselves, ==but== we did this ==because== the act was forbidden.
Behold my heart, O God, behold my heart which you had pity upon when it lay in the abyss. ==Yet== let my heart now tell you what it sought in being evil, that I should love sin for the mere sake of sin. Wickedness is foul, ==yet== I loved it. I loved to perish. I loved my own shame, ==not== the cause of my shame ==but== shame itself. I was a vile soul, abandoning your support to pursue my own ruin, seeking nothing from the shameful deed ==but== the shame itself." --- Saint Augustine, Confessions, Book II, Chapter 4.

## Connector Analysis

| Connector | Category | Argumentative Function |
|-----------|----------|------------------------|
| ==not==...==but== (1st pair) | Contrast | Denies the necessary cause of theft (need), pointing instead to the essential cause (failure of virtue, evil) |
| ==because== | Causal | Explains how the author could steal without motivation: what was stolen was already possessed, eliminating any material lack |
| ==but== | Turning Point | Further turning point, revealing that theft's sole purpose lies not in enjoying the goods, but in enjoying the theft itself |
| ==not==...==but== (2nd pair) | Contrast | In the pear tree incident, reiterates: the goal of taking fruit was not consumption but the forbidden nature of the act |
| ==but== | Turning Point | Though they tasted the fruit, denies this was the true motive, emphasizing instead the importance of the forbidden nature |
| ==because== | Causal | Establishes the fundamental motive for theft: the very forbiddenness (illegality and sinfulness) of the act |
| ==yet== | Turning Point | In contrast with God's mercy, highlights the author's fallen state |
| ==yet== | Turning Point | The critical turning point: despite wickedness being foul, the author loved it, presenting the paradox of self-awareness and self-destruction |
| ==not==...==but== (3rd pair) | Contrast | Deepens analysis of evil's nature: what is loved is not the cause of the defect but the defect itself, emphasizing purposeless, pure evil |
| ==but== (4th instance) | Contrast | Final contrast at the conclusion: the soul's destruction seeks not concealment (in shame), but rather the very exposure of shame itself |

## Preliminary Text Analysis

**Theme:** The psychological mechanism of humans committing sin without reason; evil action in the absence of motivation; the soul's love for and attachment to sin itself.

**Purpose:** In the Confessions, Augustine attempts to explain to God a motiveless theft committed in his youth, and through this, to reflect deeply on the roots of human sinfulness. He is not defending himself, but rather showing God (and the reader) the true face of evil: evil often does not arise from necessity, but from the soul's desire for transgression itself. This is a testimony to the soul's self-destruction.

**Logical Structure:**
1. Premise: Law prohibits theft, based on reason, and no one wishes to be robbed (establishing the legitimacy of universal law)
2. Contrast: Yet the author himself violates this law, not from necessity but from the soul's fallen nature
3. Argumentation: Through the concrete example of the pear tree incident, systematically stripping away material reasons to reveal the pure motive of sin
4. Deepening: Extending theft's purposelessness into love for sin itself, then extending further into the pursuit of shame itself
5. Conclusion: The soul, while recognizing God's mercy, simultaneously admits its own attachment to destruction

**Text Type:** (d) — solving a problem. Augustine addresses a fundamental moral and psychological question: why do people commit evil deeds without reason? His answer is: the soul itself has an inherent longing for sin, for transgression, for self-destruction. This is not a sociological or ethical problem, but a problem of the soul's structure itself.
