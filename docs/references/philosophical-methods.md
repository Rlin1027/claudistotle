# Philosophical Methods Reference

## Conceptual Analysis (Conceptual Analysis Method)

### When to Use

- Clarifying the meaning or nature of a concept (e.g., "What is knowledge?")
- Evaluating definitions and their adequacy
- Testing philosophical claims against intuitions and counterexamples

### Method

1. **Propose a definition**: State necessary and sufficient conditions for the concept
2. **Test with cases**: Apply to clear cases (paradigm instances), clear non-cases, and borderline cases
3. **Identify counterexamples**: Find cases that satisfy conditions but shouldn't count, or vice versa
4. **Revise the definition**: Modify to accommodate counterexamples
5. **Iterate** until reflective equilibrium is reached

### Example

```
CONCEPT: Knowledge
INITIAL DEFINITION: Knowledge = Justified True Belief
COUNTEREXAMPLE: Gettier cases (justified true belief that isn't knowledge)
REVISION: Knowledge = JTB + no defeaters / JTB + proper causal connection / ...
```

### Key Practitioners

Plato, G.E. Moore, J.L. Austin, Frank Jackson, Timothy Williamson

### Strengths & Limitations

- **Strengths**: Rigorous, produces clear claims, testable against intuitions
- **Limitations**: Relies on contested intuitions, may impose false precision on concepts that are essentially contested or family-resemblance concepts

## Hermeneutics (Hermeneutical Theory)

### When to Use

- Interpreting texts, artworks, cultural phenomena, or historical events
- Understanding meaning that is not immediately transparent
- Working with texts from different historical or cultural horizons

### Method

1. **Pre-understanding**: Acknowledge your prior assumptions and horizon (forestructure of understanding)
2. **First reading**: Encounter the text as a whole; note initial impressions and puzzles
3. **Part-whole analysis** (Hermeneutic Circle): Move between individual passages and the whole text, letting each illuminate the other
4. **Horizon fusion** (Gadamer): Allow your horizon to expand through encounter with the text's horizon
5. **Distanciation & Appropriation** (Ricoeur): Move from what the text meant (historical sense) to what it means (present application)
6. **Critical reflection**: Is your interpretation **illuminating**? Does it make more of the text intelligible than alternatives?

### Key Concepts

| Concept | German | Meaning |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Hermeneutic Circle | Hermeneutischer Zirkel | Part-whole mutual illumination |
| Horizon | Horizont | The range of vision from a particular standpoint |
| Fusion of Horizons | Horizontverschmelzung | Understanding as merging of horizons |
| Effective-Historical Consciousness | Wirkungsgeschichtliches Bewußtsein | Awareness of tradition's influence on understanding |
| Prejudice (productive) | Vorurteil | Pre-judgments that enable understanding |

### Key Practitioners

Schleiermacher, Dilthey, Heidegger (SZ §32), Gadamer, Ricoeur, Palmer

### Strengths & Limitations

- **Strengths**: Respects textual complexity, acknowledges interpreter's situatedness, productive for cross-cultural work
- **Limitations**: No single "correct" interpretation, risk of circularity, difficult to adjudicate between rival interpretations

## Phenomenology (Phenomenological Study)

### When to Use

- Describing the structure of lived experience
- Investigating consciousness, perception, embodiment, temporality
- Exploring phenomena "as they appear" before theoretical explanation

### Method

1. **Select the phenomenon**: Identify the experience to investigate (e.g., perception of time, shame, tool-use)
2. **Epochē / Bracketing**: Suspend ("bracket") assumptions about the phenomenon's causes, reality-status, or scientific explanation. Focus on **how it appears**
3. **Description**: Describe the phenomenon as precisely as possible, attending to:
   - Intentional structure (noesis/noema — act of consciousness / object as experienced)
   - Temporal structure (retention, primal impression, protention)
   - Bodily dimension (Leib vs. Körper)
4. **Eidetic variation**: Imaginatively vary features of the phenomenon to identify its **essential** (invariant) structures
5. **Constitutive analysis**: How does consciousness constitute the phenomenon's meaning?

### Key Concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Intentionality | Consciousness is always consciousness *of* something |
| Epochē | Suspension of the natural attitude |
| Noesis/Noema | Act of experiencing / object as experienced |
| Lebenswelt (Lifeworld) | Pre-theoretical lived world |
| Eidetic Reduction | Grasping essential structures through imaginative variation |
| Flesh (Chair) | Merleau-Ponty's concept of the lived body as both sensing and sensed |

### Key Practitioners

Husserl, Heidegger (early), Merleau-Ponty, Sartre, Levinas, Michel Henry

## Dialectics (Dialectical Method)

### When to Use

- Analyzing contradictions and their resolution/development
- Tracing the development of ideas, concepts, or historical formations
- Engaging with Hegel, Marx, or the Frankfurt School

### Hegelian Dialectical Method

1. **Thesis / Immediate Position**: State the initial claim or concept in its immediacy
2. **Antithesis / Negation**: Identify the internal contradiction or inadequacy that the thesis generates from within itself
3. **Synthesis / Aufhebung**: Show how the contradiction is resolved at a higher level that preserves (aufhebt) truth from both moments

> **Note**: Hegel himself rarely uses the terms "thesis-antithesis-synthesis." The movement is better described as immediacy → mediation → concrete universal, or abstract → negative → concrete.

### Marxian / Critical Dialectical Method

1. **Identify the phenomenon** in its apparent form (e.g., commodity, wage labor)
2. **Analyze its internal contradictions** (use-value vs. exchange-value)
3. **Trace its historical development** through these contradictions
4. **Reveal the social relations** concealed by the apparent form
5. **Project the tendency** toward transformation

### Key Practitioners

Plato (Socratic dialectic), Hegel, Marx, Adorno (negative dialectics), Žižek

## Critical Theory (Critical Theoretical Method)

### When to Use

- Analyzing how knowledge, concepts, or practices serve or challenge power structures
- Examining the social conditions of philosophical problems
- Working with Foucault, Frankfurt School, postcolonial thought, or feminist philosophy

### Genealogical Method (Foucault)

1. Identify a concept or practice treated as natural or universal (e.g., "madness," "sexuality," "punishment")
2. Trace its historical emergence — not as continuous progress, but as contingent, discontinuous events
3. Identify the power/knowledge nexus: how do knowledge-claims and power-relations mutually constitute each other?
4. Show the concept's contingency: it could have been otherwise
5. Open space for alternative possibilities

### Ideology Critique (Frankfurt School)

1. Identify the claim or practice presented as natural/rational/necessary
2. Analyze the social conditions that produce and sustain it
3. Show how it serves particular interests while claiming universality
4. Identify the emancipatory potential within the contradictions
5. Point toward alternatives that better realize the normative ideals being invoked

### Key Practitioners

Horkheimer, Adorno, Marcuse, Habermas, Foucault, Said, Spivak, Butler

## Analytic Methods (Analytic Philosophy Methods)

### When to Use

- Evaluating validity and soundness of arguments
- Analyzing logical structure of philosophical claims
- Working with formal or semi-formal reasoning

### Argument Reconstruction

1. **Identify the conclusion**: What is the author ultimately claiming?
2. **Identify explicit premises**: What reasons does the author give?
3. **Supply implicit premises**: What must be assumed for the argument to be valid?
4. **Formalize** (if helpful): Express in logical form (propositional, predicate, modal)
5. **Evaluate validity**: Does the conclusion follow from the premises?
6. **Evaluate soundness**: Are all premises actually true/plausible?

### Common Argument Patterns

| Pattern | Form | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Modus Ponens | If P then Q; P; ∴ Q | "If utilitarianism is correct, we should maximize welfare. Utilitarianism is correct. Therefore, we should maximize welfare." |
| Modus Tollens | If P then Q; ¬Q; ∴ ¬P | "If physicalism is true, qualia are physical. Qualia are not physical. Therefore, physicalism is false." |
| Reductio | Assume P; derive contradiction; ∴ ¬P | "Assume moral relativism. Then the Holocaust was not wrong. But the Holocaust was wrong. Therefore, moral relativism is false." |
| Dilemma | P∨Q; P→R; Q→R; ∴ R | "Either free will is compatible or incompatible with determinism. If compatible, libertarianism fails. If incompatible, hard determinism or libertarianism. Either way, the problem of free will persists." |
| Inference to Best Explanation | Evidence E; H explains E best; ∴ H is probably true | "Moral realism best explains the convergence of moral judgments across cultures. Therefore, moral realism is probably true." |

### Key Practitioners

Frege, Russell, Quine, Kripke, Lewis, Williamson

## Comparative Philosophy (Comparative Philosophical Study)

### When to Use

- Drawing insights between different philosophical traditions (e.g., Western and Chinese, Indian and Greek)
- Addressing philosophical problems through cross-cultural lenses
- Building philosophical dialogue across traditions

### Method

1. **Establish the philosophical problem**: Start with a shared problem, not a tradition
2. **Internal reconstruction**: Understand each tradition's response in its OWN terms and categories
3. **Identify structural parallels**: Where do different traditions address similar issues?
4. **Note productive differences**: Where do they diverge, and what does each divergence illuminate?
5. **Avoid these traps**:
   - **False equivalence**: "Dao = Logos" (oversimplifies both concepts)
   - **Hegemonic comparison**: Using one tradition as the standard and the other as "different from it"
   - **Cherry-picking**: Selecting only the parts that conveniently match
6. **Generate new insight**: What does the comparison reveal that neither tradition alone could show?

### Key Practitioners

Hajime Nakamura, Nishida Kitarō, Alasdair MacIntyre, Martha Nussbaum, Roger Ames, David Hall, François Jullien, Chenyang Li

### Useful Comparative Frameworks

| Western Concept | Chinese Counterpart | Key Differences |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Virtue (aretē) | De | Individual excellence vs. relational power/potency |
| Rights | Yi | Individual entitlements vs. role-based appropriateness |
| Autonomy | Ziran | Self-legislation vs. self-so-ing / naturalness |
| Truth (alētheia) | Dao / Cheng | Correspondence/disclosure vs. way/sincerity |
| Being (ousia) | You/Wu | Substance vs. presence-absence dynamic |
